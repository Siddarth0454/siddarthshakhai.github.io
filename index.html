Hands-On Science

The Laser Microscope Experiment

A Class 11 experiment that turned basic lab equipment into a working projector — and showed me what engineering actually feels like.

🔬 Dirty Water Under Laser Light — A DIY Microscope Projector

In Class 11 at Johnson Grammar School, our group designed and built a laser-based water droplet projector that could magnify microscopic impurities — including bacteria — onto a wall in real time, using nothing but a syringe, two glasses, a holder, and a laser pointer.

⚗️ The Setup

Two glass tumblers were placed side by side on a table, facing a clean white wall. A syringe filled with dirty water was held inverted between them using a stand/holder. The flow was carefully adjusted until a single droplet hung suspended at the syringe tip — not falling, just hanging.

🔦 The Physics

A laser pointer was aimed directly through the suspended water droplet toward the wall. The droplet — being a near-perfect spherical water lens — refracted and magnified the laser light. Every particle, impurity, and microorganism inside the dirty water cast a magnified shadow projection on the wall, visible to the naked eye.

🧫 What We Saw

The wall projection clearly showed particles, impurities, and microbial matter from the dirty water — magnified significantly — without any lens, microscope, or powered equipment. Just a droplet acting as a natural convex lens and a laser as the light source. Simple. Elegant. Effective.

💡 Why It Matters

This experiment demonstrated core principles of optics, refraction, and light scattering in a completely self-designed setup. It wasn't from a textbook instruction — it was conceived, built, and tested by us. That process of designing a working system from first principles is exactly the kind of thinking I want to develop further as an engineer at SIT.
